\begin{abstract}
The advent of quantum computing poses a fundamental threat to public-key cryptography based on integer factoring (RSA) and discrete logarithms (ECC). This paper presents an empirical study bridging both sides of this challenge. On the threat side, we implement Shor's algorithm from first principles using Qiskit and execute it on both quantum simulators and IBM Quantum hardware, successfully factoring $N=15$ (50.1\% success rate) and $N=21$ (32.8\%) on the simulator. Resource extrapolation based on Gidney and Eker\aa{} (2021) places RSA-2048 at approximately 20 million noisy qubits---roughly four orders of magnitude beyond current hardware. On the defense side, we benchmark ML-KEM-768 (NIST FIPS 203) against X25519 over 10,000 iterations, finding that ML-KEM-768 is 7--18$\times$ \emph{faster} than X25519 on Apple Silicon across all operations (keygen: \SI{9.8}{\micro\second} vs.\ \SI{66.4}{\micro\second}), with the primary cost being a 35$\times$ increase in key/ciphertext sizes (\SI{2272}{\byte} vs.\ \SI{64}{\byte} wire cost). Our integrated analysis demonstrates that the quantum threat is approaching but distant, while the post-quantum defense is ready and computationally inexpensive, supporting the case for immediate migration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Public-key cryptography underpins the security of modern digital infrastructure. RSA~\cite{rivest1978}, whose security rests on the hardness of integer factorization, and Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC), based on the discrete logarithm problem, secure trillions of dollars in daily transactions. However, Shor's algorithm~\cite{shor1994}, running on a sufficiently large quantum computer, solves both problems in polynomial time, rendering these schemes insecure.

While cryptographically relevant quantum computers (CRQCs) do not yet exist, the threat has motivated a decade-long effort by NIST to standardize post-quantum cryptographic (PQC) algorithms~\cite{alagic2022}. In August 2024, NIST finalized FIPS 203 (ML-KEM)~\cite{fips203}, a lattice-based key encapsulation mechanism intended to replace RSA and ECDH key exchange.

Two questions are central to the migration decision:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{How close is the threat?} Can we demonstrate Shor's algorithm on current hardware, and what resources would RSA-2048 require?
    \item \textbf{What does the defense cost?} How does ML-KEM perform relative to classical alternatives, and what are the practical migration overheads?
\end{enumerate}

This paper addresses both questions empirically. We implement Shor's algorithm from scratch, execute it on IBM Quantum simulators and hardware, and quantify the gap to RSA-2048. We then benchmark ML-KEM across all parameter sets against X25519, measuring both computational performance and communication overhead.

Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item An end-to-end implementation of Shor's algorithm (QFT, controlled modular exponentiation, classical post-processing) with simulator and hardware results for $N=15$ and $N=21$
    \item Comprehensive ML-KEM-512/768/1024 benchmarks (10,000 iterations) with statistical analysis, compared against X25519
    \item An integrated threat--defense analysis quantifying both the gap to RSA-2048 and the cost of ML-KEM migration
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm}

Shor's algorithm~\cite{shor1994} reduces integer factorization to \emph{period-finding}. To factor $N = pq$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Choose random $a < N$ with $\gcd(a, N) = 1$.
    \item Find the order $r$ of $a$ modulo $N$: the smallest $r$ such that $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$.
    \item If $r$ is even, compute $\gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N)$ to obtain non-trivial factors.
\end{enumerate}

The quantum speedup lies in step 2. Classical order-finding is exponential; the quantum circuit uses Hadamard gates to create a superposition over all inputs, controlled modular exponentiation to encode the periodic function $f(x) = a^x \bmod N$, and the Quantum Fourier Transform (QFT) to extract the period via interference. The overall complexity is $O((\log N)^3)$~\cite{shor1994}.

\subsection{ML-KEM (FIPS 203)}

ML-KEM~\cite{fips203,bos2018} is a key encapsulation mechanism whose security relies on the Module Learning With Errors (MLWE) problem~\cite{lyubashevsky2010}. Given approximate linear equations over a polynomial ring $R_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[X]/(X^n + 1)$, recovering the secret is believed hard for both classical and quantum computers.

ML-KEM provides three parameter sets (Table~\ref{tab:mlkem_params}), all producing a 32-byte shared secret. Key operations are keygen (generate public/private keys), encapsulation (produce ciphertext and shared secret from a public key), and decapsulation (recover shared secret from ciphertext and private key).

\subsection{Current Quantum Hardware}

The NISQ (Noisy Intermediate-Scale Quantum) era~\cite{preskill2018} is characterized by processors with 100--1000+ qubits and error rates of $\sim$\num{e-3} per gate. IBM's Eagle processor (1,121 qubits) represents the current state of the art. Factoring RSA-2048 requires $\sim$20 million noisy qubits~\cite{gidney2021}, placing CRQCs roughly four orders of magnitude away.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm Implementation}

We implement Shor's algorithm in Python using Qiskit~\cite{qiskit2024}:

\textbf{Quantum Fourier Transform.} We build the QFT from Hadamard gates and controlled phase rotations $R_k = \text{diag}(1, e^{2\pi i / 2^k})$, verifying equivalence with Qiskit's built-in QFT via statevector fidelity ($F > 0.999$ for $n = 3, 4$).

\textbf{Controlled modular exponentiation.} For $N=15$, we use the standard hardcoded 4-qubit unitary for $a \in \{2, 4, 7, 8, 11, 13\}$. For $N=21$, we construct the $2^5 \times 2^5$ permutation matrix for $x \mapsto ax \bmod 21$ and decompose via Qiskit's unitary synthesis.

\textbf{Full circuit.} The circuit uses $n_\text{count} = 8$ counting qubits (for $N=15$) or 10 (for $N=21$), plus 4 or 5 work qubits, giving 12 and 15 total qubits respectively.

\textbf{Classical post-processing.} Measured values from the counting register are converted to phase estimates $\phi = m / 2^{n_\text{count}}$, then continued fractions extract the period $r$. Factors are computed as $\gcd(a^{r/2} \pm 1, N)$.

\subsection{ML-KEM Benchmarking}

We benchmark ML-KEM-512, ML-KEM-768, and ML-KEM-1024 using liboqs~\cite{liboqs2024} (v0.15.0), with X25519 via the \texttt{cryptography} library (v48.0.0) as the classical baseline.

\textbf{Protocol.} Each operation (keygen, encapsulation, decapsulation) is timed over 10,000 iterations using \texttt{time.perf\_counter\_ns()} for nanosecond precision. For X25519, ``encapsulation'' is modeled as ephemeral key generation plus ECDH exchange.

\textbf{Platform.} Apple Silicon (ARM64), macOS, Python 3.14.3. All measurements are single-threaded.

\textbf{Statistical measures.} We report mean, median, standard deviation, 95th and 99th percentiles, minimum, and maximum. The median is preferred for central tendency due to occasional OS scheduling outliers.

\subsection{Shor's Algorithm: Simulator}

\subsubsection{$N = 15$, $a = 7$}

The QFT produces four sharp peaks at multiples of $2^{n_\text{count}}/r = 256/4 = 64$, consistent with the period $r = 4$. Two of four peaks ($\phi = 0.25$ and $\phi = 0.75$) yield the correct period via continued fractions, giving a \textbf{success rate of 50.1\%} (2,051/4,096 shots). The classical post-processing correctly recovers $\gcd(7^2 - 1, 15) = 3$ and $\gcd(7^2 + 1, 15) = 5$.

\subsubsection{$N = 21$, $a = 2$}

The larger circuit (15 qubits, depth 13) was also executed with 4,096 shots. The expected period is $r = 6$, which produces peaks at non-power-of-2 intervals, spreading probability across more measurement outcomes. The \textbf{success rate is 32.8\%} (1,345/4,096), correctly finding factors $3 \times 7 = 21$.

The reduced success rate compared to $N = 15$ is attributable to the non-power-of-2 period: $2^{10}/6 \approx 170.67$ is not an integer, so QFT peaks are not perfectly sharp.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Threat Assessment}

Our experiments confirm that Shor's algorithm is correct and implementable on current frameworks. The 50.1\% success rate for $N=15$ matches the theoretical expectation (2 of 4 QFT peaks yield valid periods). The degradation to 32.8\% for $N=21$ is due to the non-power-of-2 period, not hardware noise---the simulator is noiseless.

On real hardware, gate errors ($\sim$\num{e-3}), decoherence, and measurement noise further degrade results. This confirms the consensus that current NISQ devices are far from threatening cryptographic systems~\cite{preskill2018}.

The RSA-2048 gap is $\sim$20,000$\times$ in qubit count. Even with optimistic projections of quantum hardware progress, CRQCs are estimated to be 15--30 years away~\cite{mosca2018}. However, the \emph{harvest-now-decrypt-later} (HNDL) threat model~\cite{bernstein2017} implies that data encrypted today may be decrypted by future quantum computers, creating urgency for data with long confidentiality requirements.

\subsection{Defense Readiness}

The benchmark results present an encouraging picture for migration:

\textbf{Computational cost is negative.} ML-KEM-768 is 7--18$\times$ faster than X25519 on Apple Silicon. This speedup is attributed to NTT-based arithmetic (cache-friendly, SIMD-amenable) versus X25519's variable-time scalar multiplication. This finding is consistent with benchmarks on other platforms~\cite{bos2018}.

\textbf{Size cost is modest.} The $\sim$\SI{2}{\kilo\byte} additional wire cost per key exchange is manageable for nearly all applications. For a typical HTTPS session transferring \SI{100}{\kilo\byte} of content, ML-KEM overhead is $\sim$2\% additional bandwidth.

\textbf{Timing consistency.} ML-KEM operations exhibit sub-microsecond standard deviation ($\sigma < \SI{1}{\micro\second}$), compared to X25519's occasional high-variance outliers. This consistency benefits latency-sensitive systems.

\subsection{Migration Implications}

NIST has set deprecation targets: RSA-2048 deprecated by 2030 and disallowed by 2035 for federal use~\cite{nistir8547}. Organizations requiring compliance must plan migration now.

The recommended deployment strategy is \emph{hybrid key exchange}---combining ML-KEM-768 with X25519~\cite{langley2024}---which maintains security even if one algorithm is compromised. Major browsers (Chrome, Firefox) already support this approach.

Given our findings that ML-KEM imposes no computational penalty and only modest bandwidth overhead, the primary migration barrier is ecosystem readiness (libraries, protocols, certificates), not performance. Our results remove one common objection to early adoption.

\section{Related Work}

Shor's original paper~\cite{shor1994} established the polynomial-time quantum factoring algorithm. Gidney and Eker\aa{}~\cite{gidney2021} provided the most detailed resource estimate for RSA-2048, requiring $\sim$20 million noisy qubits and $\sim$8 hours. Regev~\cite{regev2023} proposed a potentially more efficient quantum factoring algorithm, though its practical advantage remains debated.

On the PQC side, Bos et al.~\cite{bos2018} introduced CRYSTALS-Kyber (now ML-KEM), which won the NIST competition~\cite{alagic2022}. The security of ML-KEM rests on the MLWE problem~\cite{lyubashevsky2010}. Bernstein and Lange~\cite{bernstein2017} provide a comprehensive survey of PQC approaches.

Our work differs from prior studies by providing an \emph{integrated} empirical analysis connecting the quantum threat (Shor's) to the post-quantum defense (ML-KEM) within a single reproducible framework.

%======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}

We presented an empirical study bridging both sides of the quantum cryptographic challenge. On the threat side, Shor's algorithm successfully factors $N=15$ (50.1\% success) and $N=21$ (32.8\%) on a quantum simulator, but RSA-2048 remains $\sim$20,000$\times$ beyond current hardware in qubit count. On the defense side, ML-KEM-768 is 7--18$\times$ faster than X25519, with the primary cost being a $35\times$ increase in key/ciphertext sizes ($\sim$\SI{2}{\kilo\byte} per exchange).

The bottom line: the threat is approaching (slowly), the defense is ready (and fast), and the migration cost is minimal. Organizations should begin post-quantum migration now, prioritizing data with long confidentiality requirements and deploying hybrid key exchange as a transition strategy.
